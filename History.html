The Origin of Cocktails

Cocktail making is the practice of mixing spirits and liquers with non-alcoholic foodstuffs, typically such as fruit juices or creams. The culture surrounding the cocktail bar experience derives from America's prohibion era 'speakeasies'; so called for the comfort and safety such illicit venues offered their clientele at time where imbibing was outlawed. Much alcohol available at this time was of desperate quality, made by improper means, as professional breweries and distilleries were closed for business. One particular phrase for these homebrews persists today; 'bathtub gin'. Because of the low quality nature of these drinks there was a neccesity to hide the harsh, unpalatable, liquids inside sweet drinks. The collection and refinement of such 'cocktails' are now known as the 'classics' of all cocktail menus, distinct from the 'tiki' collection of cocktails from the southern hemisphere, or modern classics, like the Pornstar Martini and Aperol Spritz.

Adjective

cocktail (comparative more cocktail, superlative most cocktail)

  1. (obsolete) Ostentatiously lacking in manners.
    • 1830, Sporting Magazine:
      • It looks very cocktail to be seen riding through the streets of London in a scarlet coat

Verb

cocktail (third-person singular simple present cocktail, present participle cocktailing, simple past and present participle cocktailed)

  1. (transitive) To adulterate (fuel,etc.) by mixing other substances.
  2. (transitive) To treat (a person) to cocktails.
      He dined and cocktailed her at the most exclusive bars and restaurants.
